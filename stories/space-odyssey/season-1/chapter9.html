Chapter 9: The Final Test

The atmosphere inside Vyom-1 was tense. The crew had overcome countless obstacles, but this was different. The ship’s main power systems had suffered a sudden failure, leaving them stranded in the vast void of space. Life support was dwindling, and with each passing second, their chances of survival diminished.

RoHit, however, leaned back in his seat, chewing on a protein bar. “Well, this is a problem. But hey, at least we’re not being chased by aliens… yet.”

Aditi shot him a glare. “RoHit, this is serious! The oxygen reserves are dropping, and we don’t have enough power to restart the main systems.”

“Exactly why we need to stay calm, Dr. Rao. And also why I need to finish this bar before we run out of air.” RoHit grinned.

Samar Mehta, the AI expert, was frantically typing on his console. “I’m trying to reroute auxiliary power, but it’s not enough to restart propulsion.”

Commander Vikram Patel ran a hand through his hair. “There has to be a way. We didn’t come all this way to suffocate in space.”

RoHit’s eyes flickered with mischief. “Well… if we can’t restart the ship, why not jumpstart it?”

Arjun Prasad, the chief engineer, raised an eyebrow. “What do you mean?”

“Simple. We overload the external thrusters using the emergency power cells. It’s like kicking an old scooter to start it,” RoHit said, making a kicking motion.

Aditi scoffed. “Oh sure, and if it fails, we explode. Fantastic plan, RoHit.”

RoHit shrugged. “Well, technically, we’d just drift endlessly into space, which is slightly better than exploding. But look, what other options do we have?”

The crew exchanged glances. He had a point.

With no better alternatives, they sprang into action. Arjun and Samar worked on redirecting power, while RoHit suited up for an external EVA mission to manually activate the thrusters. As he floated outside the spacecraft, tethered only by a thin safety line, he couldn’t resist singing.

“I believe I can fly~”

Aditi’s voice crackled through the comms. “RoHit, I swear, if you die making bad jokes, I will personally bring you back just to kill you again.”

“Noted, Dr. Rao,” RoHit said with a chuckle. He reached the thruster panel and began working quickly. The circuits were delicate, and one wrong move could spell disaster. His fingers danced over the controls, bypassing safety protocols in what he dubbed ‘The RoHit Shortcut.’

“Alright, ready on my mark… Three… Two… One… Ignite!”

For a moment, nothing happened. Then, a sudden surge of energy shot through the ship, and Vyom-1 jolted forward. RoHit barely had time to react before he was yanked back inside by Vikram and Arjun.

The ship’s systems flickered back to life. Oxygen levels stabilized. Propulsion was online. They were back in business.

Aditi crossed her arms, looking at RoHit. “I can’t believe that actually worked.”

RoHit gave her a mock bow. “Sometimes, you just have to kick the scooter.”

The crew erupted into laughter, relief washing over them. RoHit had done it again - not by being the smartest or the strongest, but by being exactly who he was: the wildcard, the problem-solver, the accidental hero.

As Vyom-1 resumed its course, RoHit gazed out at the stars. He had saved them all… in the most ridiculous way possible. And he wouldn’t have had it any other way.
